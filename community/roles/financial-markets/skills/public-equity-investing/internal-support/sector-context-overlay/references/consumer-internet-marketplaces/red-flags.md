# Red Flags And Failure Modes

Deferred reference for `Consumer Internet Marketplaces Sector Analysis`. Load this only when the task needs this level of sector detail.

## Red flags and common traps

- GMV grows while active buyers, orders, or room nights stagnate because ticket size or FX did the work.
- Revenue grows faster than GMV due mainly to fee increases or ad monetization while core liquidity weakens.
- Supply counts rise but utilization or sell-through falls, signaling worse liquidity.
- Margins improve because incentives were cut, only to be followed by weaker growth later.
- Paid marketing rises while management emphasizes strong user growth without acknowledging worsening organic demand.
- Travel-platform revenue improves because of merchant-mix shift or cash timing, not because demand strengthened.
- Delivery or mobility GOV growth looks strong while contribution margin stalls due to insurance, labor, or routing costs.
- Ads or fintech become the story because the core marketplace is maturing or weakening.
- GMV or GOV metrics change definitions and are treated as comparable to prior periods.
- Working-capital float is mistaken for clean, repeatable free cash flow.
- Hybrid first-party and third-party models are valued like pure asset-light marketplaces.
- Fraud, counterfeits, returns, refunds, or chargebacks are understated in the debate.
- Regulatory exposure around labor classification, travel regulation, consumer protection, competition, or privacy is dismissed as noise.
- One category, one geography, or one large merchant drives an outsized share of growth and risk.
## Never do this

- Never value all marketplaces on EV / GMV.
- Never compare monthly active users to trailing-twelve-month active buyers without adjustment.
- Never assume GMV growth means share gains before normalizing for price, mix, and FX.
- Never treat ad revenue as proof of marketplace health if order frequency, conversion, or seller ROI are worsening.
- Never ignore multi-homing, off-platform leakage, or disintermediation risk.
- Never treat working-capital float as the same thing as structurally high margins.
- Never let a management narrative about network effects substitute for proof of local density and repeat behavior.
