# Archetypes And Classification

Deferred reference for `Oil Gas E&P Sector Analysis`. Load this only when the task needs this level of sector detail.

## First classify the E&P company

Classify the company before analyzing it. Do not compare all E&Ps mechanically.

## Archetypes

### Oil-weighted shale consolidator
- Core engines: short-cycle development, repeatable manufacturing, high well cadence, scale benefits, M&A optionality.
- Primary watch items: inventory tiering, well costs, type-curve durability, parent-child interference, oil differentials, corporate capital allocation.
- Common trap: paying a premium for "scale" when the real story is shrinking tier-one inventory.

### Gas-weighted Appalachia or Haynesville producer
- Core engines: dry-gas reservoir quality, firm transport portfolio, basis management, LNG and seasonal demand leverage, lower lifting costs.
- Primary watch items: basis and transport, gathering contracts, hedge policy, methane and emissions intensity, maintenance capital versus strip.
- Common trap: underwriting on Henry Hub alone and ignoring basis, transportation expense, and seasonal cash-flow volatility.

### Liquids-rich multi-basin unconventional producer
- Core engines: commodity diversification, basin optionality, capital flexibility, mix optimization.
- Primary watch items: oil versus gas versus NGL margin, basin competition for capital, corporate overhead, non-operated exposure.
- Common trap: using blended BOE metrics that hide a low-quality molecule mix.

### Offshore or deepwater independent
- Core engines: large long-cycle projects, lower base decline after start-up, higher upfront development, project concentration.
- Primary watch items: project timing, uptime, working-interest structure, geopolitical and fiscal terms, decommissioning, concentration risk.
- Common trap: applying shale-style quarter-to-quarter heuristics to a project business with lumpy start-ups and lower but less flexible decline.

### International conventional independent
- Core engines: conventional reservoir life, PSC or royalty/tax complexity, country exposure, infrastructure access.
- Primary watch items: fiscal regime, repatriation, local pricing, lifting cost stability, geopolitical risk, contract renewal.
- Common trap: comparing headline multiples directly with U.S. onshore names without adjusting for sovereign and fiscal complexity.

### Mature conventional / decline-managed consolidator
- Core engines: low-decline production, acquired PDP cash flow, optimization of legacy fields, cash harvesting.
- Primary watch items: abandonment liability, reserve revisions, workover burden, water handling, field-life extension assumptions.
- Common trap: mistaking lower headline capex for structural superiority when value is being harvested from a declining asset base.

### Small-cap exploration / appraisal / emerging-basin story
- Core engines: resource delineation, appraisal success, development funding, strategic optionality.
- Primary watch items: funding runway, risked NAV, drilling catalysts, farm-outs, regulatory approvals, convertibility from contingent resource to reserves.
- Common trap: treating unrisked resource numbers like proved value.

### Non-operated-heavy E&P
- Core engines: acreage position, partner-operated development, lean overhead, optionality across operators.
- Primary watch items: operator timing control, cost inflation pass-through, schedule uncertainty, partner quality, cash-call burden.
- Common trap: modeling volume ramps as if management controls the development schedule.

Pure mineral and royalty companies are not the main target of this skill. Use only if the underwriting question is still mostly about operator-like reserve and basin exposure rather than pure royalty economics.
