# Marketplace Modeling Rules

Deferred reference for `Consumer Internet Marketplaces Sector Analysis`. Load only when the task needs sector modeling detail.

## Core Frame

Underwrite consumer internet and marketplace issuers as transaction networks. Value depends on repeat demand, scarce-side supply, local/category liquidity, trust, monetization quality, contribution economics, cash conversion, and security claims, not reported revenue growth alone.

Start with what is being matched, booked, delivered, or sold; who pays; whether the scarce side is demand or supply; how GMV/GMS/GOV/bookings convert to revenue and cash; whether cohorts repeat without subsidies; whether take rate rises for durable reasons; and whether working-capital float or restricted cash flatters liquidity.

Treat a beat as low quality when driven by temporary incentive cuts, ticket inflation, travel normalization, gross-versus-net accounting, agency/merchant mix, favorable FX, one-time cancellations/refunds behavior, or ad load that raises revenue while core liquidity weakens.

## Analysis Sequence

### 1) Transaction architecture and unit of account
- Identify the core unit: order, item, room night, trip, booking, service hour, lead, listing, or transaction.
- Bridge GMV/GMS/GOV/gross bookings to recognized revenue, contribution profit, operating cash flow, and security value.
- Split monetization into transaction fees, seller/buyer fees, ads, memberships, payments, financing, shipping, insurance, fulfillment, and other services.
- Determine who pays each fee, what is mandatory versus optional, and whether revenue recognition timing or cancellation windows distort growth.
- Do not compare gross and net revenue models without normalizing pass-throughs, taxes, tips, shipping, refunds, and incentives.

### 2) Liquidity, density, and match quality
- Analyze liquidity by geography, category, cohort, and use case; total network scale can hide weak local density.
- Determine whether buyers get selection, availability, speed, and trust, and whether sellers/providers earn enough volume to remain active.
- Track fill rate, conversion, sell-through, response time, utilization, cancellations, refunds, defects, and successful-match rate where available.
- Growth purchased in immature markets deserves a lower quality score than growth from better conversion and mature-market repeat behavior.
- Multi-homing and easy disintermediation weaken network effects even when headline scale looks large.

### 3) Demand acquisition, cohorts, and repeat behavior
- Build demand from new users, reactivated users, repeat users, frequency, AOV/ADR, category expansion, and geography.
- Separate direct traffic, SEO, app, referrals, brand, and paid acquisition; rising paid mix can signal weaker organic pull.
- Cohorts should be cut by acquisition channel, geography, category, and first-order month where possible.
- Frequency gains and category expansion usually matter more than one-time ticket-size spikes.
- Reactivation is attractive only when reactivated cohorts keep spending after incentives normalize.

### 4) Supply quality and partner economics
- Identify the supply-side actor: seller, merchant, host, courier, driver, provider, lister, or professional service partner.
- Test onboarding friction, time to first transaction, supply retention, concentration, multi-homing, utilization, and average earnings.
- Separate supply count growth from supply productivity; more listings with lower utilization can signal weaker liquidity.
- Fee increases and ad monetization are durable only if supply ROI and retention remain healthy.
- Normalize incentives, fee rebates, marketing credits, and support costs needed to recruit or retain supply.

### 5) GMV growth and take-rate quality
- Decompose GMV/bookings into active-demand growth, frequency, volume units, AOV/ADR, category mix, geography, FX, inflation, acquisitions, and divestitures.
- Separate core transaction take rate from ads, subscriptions, payments, fulfillment, financing, and other ancillary layers.
- Rising take rate is good only if liquidity, repeat behavior, trust, and supply economics remain healthy.
- Ads are high quality when they follow buyer intent and seller ROI; excessive ad load can tax sellers and degrade conversion.
- For OTAs, merchant-versus-agency mix can change revenue and cash timing without a true demand change.

### 6) Contribution economics, trust, and fulfillment
- Build contribution profit after promotions, payment processing, support, refunds, chargebacks, insurance, logistics, trust and safety, and merchant/driver support.
- Distinguish order acquisition from order fulfillment and from fixed platform cost.
- Density, routing, ML matching, and better fraud controls should lower variable cost per transaction; complexity can offset scale.
- Asset-light narratives are weak if refunds, fraud, insurance, support, compliance, or logistics intensity are structurally rising.
- Margin improvement from lower incentives is less durable than improvement from liquidity, automation, and cost-per-order leverage.

### 7) Cash flow, working capital, and balance sheet
- Reconcile FCF with customer funds, restricted cash, unearned revenue, seller/merchant/host payables, payment reserves, accrued incentives, leases, and SBC dilution.
- Stable working-capital float can be real but is not the same as high incremental margins; it reverses when GMV slows.
- Customer custodial balances and restricted cash are not discretionary liquidity.
- Review convert maturities, capped calls, warehouse facilities, regulated entities, insurance reserves, guarantees, and legal contingencies.
- For debt instruments, route underwriting to Credit Markets; retain downside cash generation, fixed-cost flexibility, working-capital reversal, and marketplace liquidity only as common-equity risk context.

### 8) Moat and security value
- Determine whether the moat comes from local liquidity, brand, trust, unique supply, logistics, data, habit, direct traffic, or regulatory position.
- Test dependence on app stores, search, social platforms, paid acquisition, and third-party logistics or payments.
- Category expansion creates value only when user overlap, supply leverage, and monetization logic are real.
- Equity rerates need proof of stronger cohorts, liquidity, take-rate durability, contribution margin, and moat depth.
- A good marketplace can still be a bad security if value depends on multiple expansion, vendor float, dilution, or fragile legal/regulatory assumptions.
