# Red Flags And Failure Modes

Deferred reference for `Bank Sector Analysis`. Load this only when the task needs this level of sector detail.

## Red flags and common traps

Treat these as high-priority warning signals:
- loan growth meaningfully outpacing core deposit growth
- margin upside driven by risky asset mix, temporary accretion, or lagged liability repricing that is about to reverse
- heavy reliance on brokered, promotional, or concentrated uninsured funding
- reserve releases despite worsening leading indicators
- large office or other concentrated exposures with stale appraisals and limited disclosure
- capital ratio comfort that disappears once OCI, RWA inflation, or stress losses are incorporated
- buybacks, M&A, or dividend promises made from a thin true capital cushion
- management guidance that highlights NII but avoids deposit migration, hedges, or funding mix
- “strong liquidity” language unsupported by monetizable asset quality
- holdco debt or preferreds treated casually without entity-level analysis
- efficiency improvements coming from under-spending on controls, remediation, or franchise investment
- fast balance-sheet growth at an online or niche lender without evidence of durable funding
## Never do this

- Do not lead with EBITDA, EV/EBITDA, or generic FCF language.
- Do not compare banks on P/TBV without comparing normalized ROTCE and tail risk.
- Do not celebrate NIM expansion without decomposing yield, beta, remix, hedges, and credit quality.
- Do not treat reserve releases as earnings quality by default.
- Do not discuss capital without asking what the binding constraint is.
- Do not write on bank debt or preferreds without naming the issuer, entity, and ranking.
- Do not compare efficiency ratios across very different bank archetypes without adjustment.
- Do not assume reported regulatory capital equals economic capital strength.
- Do not let period-end funding or capital snapshots substitute for average-balance earnings analysis.
