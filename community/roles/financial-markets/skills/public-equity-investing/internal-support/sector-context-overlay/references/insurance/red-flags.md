# Red Flags And Failure Modes

Deferred reference for `Insurance Sector Analysis`. Load this only when the task needs this level of sector detail.

## Red flags and common traps

Treat these as high-priority warning signals:
- premium growth outpacing rate adequacy or capital growth
- favorable prior-year development masking weak current accident-year underwriting
- casualty reserve releases despite worsening social inflation or claim severity
- catastrophe-exposed writer with thin retrocession or optimistic PML assumptions
- life or annuity earnings propped up by illiquid asset reach, alternative marks, or lagged crediting resets
- holdco buybacks or dividends funded from thin liquid resources or unsustainably high remittances
- aggressive affiliated reinsurance or reserve financing used to manufacture capital optics
- large CRE, lower-rated structured, or private-asset exposures with weak disclosure
- ratings pressure ignored because RBC looks optically high
- management steering attention to adjusted earnings while avoiding reserve or capital disclosures
- conglomerate complexity used to bury a weak business line
- runoff or ADC benefits mistaken for true franchise improvement
## Never do this

- Do not lead with EBITDA, EV/EBITDA, or generic revenue-growth language.
- Do not compare combined ratios without normalizing for catastrophe load, prior-year development, mix, and reserve quality.
- Do not accept reserve releases as high-quality earnings by default.
- Do not analyze life insurers without separating spread, fee, hedge, and capital generation.
- Do not discuss debt or preferreds without mapping the exact issuing entity and structural subordination.
- Do not treat RBC or solvency ratios as the only capital constraint.
- Do not compare premiums, deposits, and IFRS 17 insurance revenue as if they are the same thing.
- Do not annualize a cat-light year or a temporarily favorable spread environment.
- Do not ignore reinsurance recoverable and counterparty risk.
- Do not let reported EPS substitute for reserve, asset, and capital analysis.
