# Red Flags And Failure Modes

Deferred reference for `Biotech Pharma Sector Analysis`. Load this only when the task needs this level of sector detail.

## Red flags and common traps

- Management spotlights a favorable subgroup after the primary endpoint missed.
- The dataset is statistically positive but clinically weak or commercially narrow.
- Follow-up is short, censoring is heavy, or the control arm underperformed versus current standard of care.
- Management uses platform language to imply replication before any second asset shows real human proof.
- A rare-disease launch looks strong because of pent-up demand and easy-to-find patients, but persistence or new-patient flow weakens quickly.
- Gross-to-net rises while reported net sales still grow, masking worsening pricing power.
- Collaboration or milestone revenue hides weak product demand or heavy cash burn.
- Manufacturing readiness lags the clinical narrative, especially for advanced modalities.
- A product appears approvable, but the likely label, REMS, or monitoring burden sharply lowers real uptake.
- Patent protection is weaker than the narrative suggests because secondary patents dominate the estate.
- The company sells future economics through royalty monetization to avoid an equity raise, reducing long-term upside and adding senior claims.
- Cash runway does not comfortably reach the next real inflection point.
- The company depends on one partner, one wholesaler channel, one supplier, or one trial readout more than the headline story implies.
- A large-cap pharma company buys revenue to bridge a patent cliff, but the deal economics or integration burden make replacement value poor.
- Analysts compare ORR, PFS, or launch growth across names without normalizing for line, label, follow-up, or gross-to-net.
## Never do this

- Never treat orphan designation, Breakthrough Therapy, or Priority Review as proof that the asset will work or the label will be broad.
- Never equate statistical significance with clinical relevance or commercial value.
- Never annualize one quarter of milestone revenue, stocking, or government orders into durable product economics.
- Never compare response rates or survival curves across trials without checking population and design.
- Never use list price as if it were realized price.
- Never assume approval automatically means smooth reimbursement or rapid uptake.
- Never ignore CMC and inspection risk for biologics, cell therapy, gene therapy, or sterile manufacturing.
- Never assume patents and regulatory exclusivities are the same thing.
- Never value a cash-box biotech without explicitly modeling burn, dilution, and time to financing.
- Never forget that debt, converts, royalties, and liens change downside even when the science is attractive.
